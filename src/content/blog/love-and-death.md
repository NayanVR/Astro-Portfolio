### I wanted to write about the death but it would be too dark so this is what i ended up writing on death, attachment, and the contract you already signed.

Somewhere right now, a flood is happening. Or an earthquake. Or a war that has been going on long enough. Thousands of people are dying in it. You know this because it is in the news, sitting between an advertisement for a less fat chips and a cover of your favourtie song. You look at the number. You register it, the way you register a temperature reading, informationally, correctly, and without much feeling. Then you scroll.

Not because you are a bad person. This is important to establish early, because the article that begins with "you scrolled past thousands of deaths" tends to be the kind of article that ends by making you feel terrible about yourself, and that is not what this is. You scrolled because you are human, and humans are not equipped to feel a number. The brain was built for faces. It was built for the particular person sitting across the table, not for the abstraction of a casualty count.

This is not a moral failing. It is a design specification.

Psychologists call it psychic numbing, the phenomenon where emotional response to death does not scale with scale. The feeling peaks somewhere around one. Maybe two. A single named child reported on the news with photographs and updates generates more grief than a famine affecting millions (like you forgot famine of china or you never knew). The math of suffering runs in reverse: the larger the number, the smaller the feeling. The mind, confronted with mass death, does not grieve harder. It goes quiet.

Quote originated in 1924 from French satirical paper Comic-Finance "death of one man is terrible, 100k deaths is a statistic". It was not, for once, being merely cold. I feel they were being accidentally precise about the architecture of human empathy. We feel the one. The million becomes information.

Which raises the question that this whole article is, at its core, trying to sit with:

> If death only becomes real when it is personal, what is the thing that makes it personal?

---

The thing that makes a death land, that makes it not a statistic but an event, not information but a rupture is _love_. Specifically: whether you loved the person who died. Whether they had been woven into the ordinary fabric of your daily life. Whether their absence now shows up everywhere, in the wrong side of the bed and the tea made for two (because who likes coffee) and the joke you started telling before you remembered there was no one left to finish it for.

Death, on its own, is a biological fact. Cells stop. Organs follow. The body, which had been maintaining itself against considerable odds for decades, decides to stop maintaining itself. This happens to every living thing, constantly, at a rate of roughly 1.8 people per second globally. An average person reads at 238 WPM, which means by the time you reached here 237 people died. It is among the most ordinary events in the universe.

It only becomes extraordinary when love is involved. I'm heavily inspired by Varun Grover to write this bit so special thanks to him.

---

Here is what love actually is, before we dress it up.

It starts releasing oxytocin, a bonding hormone triggered by sustained closeness, physical touch, the simple fact of someone being reliably there. Researchers have named it the "love drug" which is accurate in every way except the ways it would be inconvenient for it to be accurate.

You did not, in any meaningful sense, choose this. You were in proximity.

> Most of the freinds you have ever had, are mostly because of geographical proximity.

The chemistry followed. The attachment accumulated the way interest accumulates, quietly, invisibly, until one day you check the balance and realize how much is at stake.

Over time, the brain encodes this person into its reward circuitry. Their presence becomes an expected input. Their voice, their patterns, the specific texture of their company, all of it gets written into what the brain expects to receive. And when the brain expects something and does not receive it, it registers the absence as distress. Not metaphorically. Actually. Neurologically.

### What you call "missing someone" is, at the level of neurons, closer to withdrawal. The brain scanning for a stimulus it has been trained to need, finding nothing, generating the signal that something is wrong. Because something is wrong. The drug is gone.

Grief, properly understood, is a pharmacological condition. It is the body recalibrating after the removal of something it had organized itself around. The sadness is real. The love was real. But the mechanism is sometimes poetic and majorly it is biological, measurable, and largely outside your control.

You didn't decide to love them enough that their death would matter. Your nervous system decided, gradually, through accumulated exposure. The grief is the invoice for the attachment. You didn't choose the attachment so much as you chose the proximity and the rest followed automatically, the way a contract activates once you've signed without reading it.

The strangers in the flood never got that close. So their deaths, however real, however total, arrive in you as news rather than rupture. This is not callousness. This is oxytocin, distributed unevenly across a life, doing exactly what it was designed to do.

---

Now here is where the argument turns, and becomes either comforting or more disturbing, depending on your temperament.

Knowing all of this, knowing that love is a slow neurochemical accumulation, that grief is withdrawal, that your capacity to feel a death is entirely proportional to your prior exposure to the person who died, you would think the rational response would be restraint. Careful management of proximity. Deliberate limitation of oxytocin debt. A sensible portfolio approach to human attachment, hedged against catastrophic loss.

You would be wrong.

In the 1980s, two psychologists named Jeff Greenberg and Sheldon Solomon began working on a theory that would eventually be called Terror Management Theory. The premise was straightforward and slightly vertiginous: humans are the only animals with full knowledge of their own mortality. We know, with complete certainty, that we will die. We know that everyone we love will die. We know the whole project is finite, that the curtain comes down, that the story ends. And this knowledge, this specific, unavoidable, always-in-the-background awareness - should, by any reasonable calculation, produce a species in a state of constant existential paralysis.

It does not. And the reason it does not is one of the more quietly astonishing things about being human.

When people are reminded of death, even subliminally, even through something as minor as walking past a funeral home or answering a survey question about mortality, they do not retreat from connection. They move toward it. They invest more in relationships. They hold people closer. They seek meaning with greater urgency. The awareness of the limit does not drain the experience of its value. It intensifies it.

The exit sign, it turns out, is precisely what makes the room feel worth being in.

Which means the relationship between love and death is not oppositional. It is not two forces pulling in opposite directions, one trying to preserve while the other destroys. It is more like a single system with two functions, each one making the other possible, each one requiring the other to mean anything at all.

Death without love is a statistic. You scroll past it.

Love without death is a hypothesis. Untested, unweighted, without the pressure that makes it feel like anything.

It is only when they meet, when you have loved someone long enough that your neurons have restructured around them, and then that person is gone, that either concept achieves its full, devastating resolution.

---

So here is the situation, stated plainly.

To feel death, really feel it, not as news but as loss, you must first have loved. And to have loved, you must have gotten close. And getting close means the oxytocin accumulated. And the oxytocin accumulating means the withdrawal is coming. The grief is built into the love the way the hangover is built into the drink. You cannot have one without eventually having the other. The system does not offer a version without the cost.

And here is the part that Terror Management Theory discovered and that everyone who has ever loved anyone already knows in their bones: you will do it anyway. Not despite the knowledge of what it costs. The awareness of the cost is, neurologically, part of what makes the investment feel worth making. You lean into love more fiercely, not less, when you remember that it ends. The limit is the thing that makes it feel real.

You are, in other words, a creature that cannot feel the deaths of strangers, can only feel the deaths of those you have loved, and responds to the awareness of death by loving people harder. The entire architecture is oriented around a loss that hasn't happened yet. Every attachment is, simultaneously, a pre-scheduled grief.

You know this. Everyone knows this. You do it anyway.

Enthusiastically, even.

---

Death has no meaning without love. This is true. What is also true, and harder to sit with, is that love has no weight without death, that the thing you are most afraid of losing is made precious, in part, by the fact that you can lose it. That the grief you are dreading is the proof that the love was real. That the contract you signed, the one with the unreadable date in the corner, was signed with full knowledge that it would eventually be called in.

You signed it anyway.

What do you make of a creature like that?
