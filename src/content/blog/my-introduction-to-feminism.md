Speaking of priviledge, the base of it is education. Let me give you a simple scenario. There is a family in indian household with 3-4 kids. They don't have enough finances to educate them all because come on who think about that. No offence to parents but it is the only designation that requies no qualification. So coming back to the point. What do you think who will left out. Now multiply that planning across enough households, across enough decades, and you get a number. India's female literacy rate currently sits at 64.63%. The male rate is 80.88%.

> 17.2 Percentage points separating male and female literacy in India, a gap that earned the country its 124th rank in educational attainment globally, per the World Economic Forum's 2024 Gender Gap Report.

Today, according to the government's own Periodic Labour Force Survey, 43% of women outside the workforce cite childcare and domestic responsibilities as the primary barrier. After all of this I don't feel we should honour of Devi to out women, just treat them like humans first and I feel half of the problem will be gone. I feel a bit upset when I see majority woman follow their religion which was designed to suppress them or limit them to something more than household chores.

In 1848, a woman named Savitribai Phule walked to school every morning carrying an extra sari.

She needed it because, on the way, she would be pelted with stones and cow dung. She was walking to teach girls how to read. The people throwing things were not strangers. They were her neighbors. Some of them were women.

She said, afterwards, that the dung felt like flowers. This is either the most radical thing a person has ever said, or a very effective way to make your abusers feel stupid. Possibly both.

> "I am doing a sacred task of teaching my young sisters, the stones pelted at me are like flowers."

The system does not need villains to run. This is the part that takes the longest to understand. The women throwing stones were not confused about what they were doing. They were protecting **a social order that had given them the only form of security they knew how to access**. You can be imprisoned for asking questions without endorsing it. You can enforce a rule you hate because breaking it costs more than following it. To call her a hypocrite is easy. To understand what she was given to work with is harder and more honest.

Here is what feminism is not: **the idea that a woman should be able to do everything a man can do.**

That framing is flattering to men, if you think about it. It positions their range of available choices as the finish line. Be a CEO. Run a country. Fix the engine etc etc etc. Congratulations, you've matched the standard.

What feminism actually is, once you strip away the decades of argument about what it is: **the idea that a woman should be able to do whatever she wants to do.** Including things no man has ever done or been asked to do. Including being ordinary. Including being mediocre. Including being nothing remarkable at all, and having that be fine.

The question is what you were looking at before and whether you chose not to see, or simply never had to.
